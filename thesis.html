Master's Thesis

Deriving True Lensing Signals by Minimizing Shape Noise Using JWST Multi-band Observations

This project was part of my M.S. in Physics at Drexel University, conducted under the supervision of Dr. David Goldberg. The goal was to utilize multi-band flexion measurements to minimize intrinsic shape noise and obtain a more accurate reconstruction of the matter distribution in galaxy clusters.

In weak gravitational lensing, the light from distant galaxies is distorted by massive intervening structures. The first-order distortion, known as shear, causes galaxies to appear slightly stretched, while the second-order distortion, called flexion, captures subtle curvature or bending in their light profiles. Flexion is particularly sensitive to small-scale variations in the lensing potential, making it a powerful but noise-sensitive probe of substructure within galaxy clusters.

In principle, the induced flexion depends solely on the mass of the deflector and should appear identical across different photometric bands. However, because galaxies exhibit distinct morphologies across band-passes, the measured flexion varies—introducing shape noise. To mitigate this, I employed the multi-band imaging capability of the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), whose wide set of filters allows precise cross-band analysis of individual galaxy shapes.

Data and Pipeline

The galaxy clusters SMACS J0723 and Abell 2744 were chosen as test-beds because of their well-studied lensing environments and high-quality JWST imaging data. I acquired Level-2 images from the MAST portal, corresponding to calibrated exposures of different cluster regions in multiple NIRCam filters.

A three-stage Python pipeline was developed:

  • Stage 1: Utilized the official JWST pipeline to align World Coordinate Systems (WCS), mosaic multi-band exposures, and resample them onto a common pixel grid.
  • Stage 2: Employed the JWST source catalog to generate postage stamps for each galaxy, which were then analyzed with LENSER to estimate shear and flexion components.
  • Stage 3: Implemented Kaiser & Squires' Fourier inversion technique to derive convergence maps from shear data and the BGRT Fourier method to reconstruct convergence from flexion measurements.

Results and Impact

By combining flexion measurements across multiple bands, the approach effectively reduced intrinsic shape noise and produced convergence maps that better reflected the true underlying mass distribution. The resulting shear- and flexion-based convergence fields showed consistent structural features, suggesting the reliability of the multi-band approach for future lensing analyses. This work highlights JWST's unique ability to refine weak lensing precision on small scales.
